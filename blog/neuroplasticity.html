Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

By understanding neuroplasticity, people can use strategies to improve Neuroplasticity in their brain and build strong neural pathways. This allows effective learning of new skills or information.

By the time we’ve passed our late twenties, the number of synapses firing between the neuron's in our brain have greatly reduced since childhood. A synapse is the small gap at the end of a neuron that allows information to transfer from one neuron to the next. The process of these reducing is called ‘Synaptic pruning’. The brain strengthens pathways in the brain that are used often and eliminates pathways that haven’t aren’t used. This is where the saying comes from: ‘neurons that fire together, wire together.’ The more we focus on one particular area of learning the stronger we can build neural pathways in this area!

The way I will use Neuroplasticity to my benefit is by practicing coding consistently to create strong neural pathways in this area. I’m also concentrating on doing a wider range of exercise at the moment, by taking small achievable steps towards my exercise goals I’ll slowly but surely create strong neural pathways that allow for more efficient learning around exercise.

Some of the more generic ways to increase neuroplasticity in the brain are:

  1. Getting lots of sleep. Sleep effects dendritic growth. Dendrites help with the transmission of information from one nueron to the next. The quality of your alertness is dependent on your sleep quality for the past two nights.
  2. Exercising often. Exercise can prevent neuron loss in the hippocampus (involved holding short term memories and transferring them to short term memory)
  3. Doing Mindfulness practices.

Some quite unique strategies suggested by Dr. Andrew Huberman are:

  1. Cyclical breathing: taking 10-15 vigorous breathes will trigger alertness.
  2. Visual focus exercise: Sit and focus your eyes on one specific location in the room, when your eyes start to shift from this location, re set your focus on the area again. Doing this for 15 to 60 seconds is effective for focus when learning from a book or screen. Your cognitive focus follows your visual focus.
  3. Generate as many repetitions possible of a particular task in a short period of time.The brain recognises this and triggers neural plasticity. If you feel hurried, you’re doing it right! This practise will allow you to perform the action without even having to try.
  4. Expect and embrace errors, these signal to your nervous system that something needs to change in your brain. The second repetition of a task after an error will be far more effective.
  5. Micro-rest intervals: random breaks where we stop doing the activity for 10 seconds. Science shows our neurons keep firing but at a 10x faster rate. It’s important that these rest occur randomly and for no longer than 10 seconds.

Growth Mindset

Based on our upbringing and life experiences we all carry a belief about our abilities and this belief can fit into one of two categories, Fixed or Growth. An individual with a fixed mindset doesn’t believe they can change their abilities, personality, relationships or lifestyle etc. They believe that what has been cemented in the past, such as their intelligence, is rigidly in place. This causes issues for these people as they go throughout life. A person with a fixed mindset can be easily identified through five key symptoms:

  • How they approach challenges. A fixed mindset will avoid challenges, while a growth mindset will embrace them.
  • How they work through obstacles. A fixed mindset will give up easily, a growth mindset will employ persistence.
  • Their perception of effort. A fixed mindset will see effort as pointless when their mind ‘doesn’t have the natural ability to achieve the challenge set out.’ A growth mindset will see all effort as a stepping stone on the journey to mastery.
  • How they react to criticism. A fixed mindset will disregard constructive criticism and will take it as a personal attack on their own intelligence. A growth mindset will learn from criticism.
  • How they feel about the success of others. A fixed mindset will feel threatened by the success of others. A growth mindset will find inspiration and will learn lessons through the success of others.

They study of adopting a growth mindset when learning to code is relevant as it’s a very difficult thing to learn. If an individual was to tackle the task of becoming a programmer while avoiding challenges, giving up in the face of obstacles, seeing their effort as pointless, reacting negatively to criticism and feeling threatened by the success of others… their success would be minimised.

Something that surprised me was that I identified areas in my own life where I have a fixed mindset. I’ve always had the belief that mindset is everything and that we can achieve anything if we employ an open or growth mindset. However, through my time working in the marketing industry these highly optimistic views were destroyed. If you’re in a toxic work environment that doesn’t support you, no matter how much you embrace challenges, persist through obstacles and take on criticism as opportunity to learn and be inspired, you’ll eventually feel like your efforts are fruitless.

Reading these articles has allowed me to regain my optimism and growth mindset that I didn’t know I’d long lost. What a life changing Monday morning!

I’ll be using a growth mindset in my own learning journey by following the small mantra I’ve written below. I’ve written this on a piece of paper near my desk so I can consistently move my mind toward a growth mindset.

Growth Mindset Mantra

Run towards challenges.
Persist through set backs.
All effort is a worthy investment.
Welcome criticism and learn from it.
Celebrate the success of others and take inspiration from them.

It would also be interesting to see whether this kind of mantra can be combined with my mindfulness practises to create new neural pathways.
